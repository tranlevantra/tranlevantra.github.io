🌏 Work Experience

I began as an Operations Intern at Toyota Vietnam, working in the warehouse to ensure spare parts arrived on time, in the right place, quantity, and condition. Maybe I’m biased, but Toyota’s low-tech warehouse management system — Kanban(paper cards) — could manage complex workflows better than any fancy software.

Later, I joined BSS Group as a Manual Software Tester, writing and executing test plans. As someone new to software development, I was lucky to have incredibly patient and supportive mentors. If I ever get the chance to mentor someone, I’ll strive to be the same.

Both roles were detail-focused and taught me patience. But I craved something more analytical and math-driven — so I pursued a graduate degree in Statistics, right after completing my Business degree.

🧠 MATH

I solve math problems in my free time — I’m a nerd, no doubt! The kind of math I study is statistics — not pure math, but a mathematical science nonetheless. I feel incredibly grateful to study math at this level. And as cliché as it sounds: the more I learn, the more I realize how little I actually know.

Because AI and machine learning are trending — and statistics is their backbone — here are some resources on the math of data science (tested and approved by a nerd 🤓):

💻 CODING

I’m still new to coding — and feel so lucky that auto-completion, Copilot, and ChatGPT were already around when I began.

My favorite language so far is Java — it’s declarative, easy to read, and has just the right amount of strictness to catch bugs early. But the language I’m most fluent in is R, because I study Statistics — and R was developed by statisticians, for statisticians 📈.

Arguing about which programming language is “better” feels a bit pointless to me. Languages are tools, and each serves a different purpose. What matters is what you build with them.

I do wish I’d taken Data Structures and Algorithms in school — It’s all good, there’s an abundance of amazing online resources now. 📚💻
